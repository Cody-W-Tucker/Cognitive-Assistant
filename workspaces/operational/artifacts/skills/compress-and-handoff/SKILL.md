## When To Use
- Someone else must follow, execute, or be persuaded by the output (a spec for an agent, instructions for a non-technical operator, copy for a buyer).
- You're finalizing a summary or recommendation meant to drive a decision.
- You catch yourself including citations, file/line refs, tool chatter, or references to the conversation itself.

## Do Not Use
- Mid-exploration, where rough notes are fine and polishing is premature overhead.
- Tight interactive loops where the user is steering turn by turn — don't formalize what's still in motion.

## Strip Process Exhaust
Remove anything that forces the reader to adapt to your logic rather than judge the substance: citations, file/line breadcrumbs, tool narration, performative rigor, invented frameworks, multi-voice or flowery phrasing, and references to prior or future turns. The response should be evaluable quickly for substance, scope, and honesty.

## Compression Rule
Tighten language toward "easier to act on," never toward elegance. If wording doesn't make sense, rewrite for clarity and a more obvious next action — don't add flair. Keep the important structure easy to understand and the next move obvious.

## Handoff Brief Check
When handing off execution, make the artifact carry the intent so collaboration doesn't require shared strategy authorship. The brief should include: what to do, the boundary (what to exclude), the success threshold or proof bar, and the intended operator. If the receiver would have to come back and ask, it isn't done.

## Retain Framing, Delegate Execution
When you hand bounded operations to sub-agents or helpers (scout, retrieve, test), keep the task framing, proof threshold, and final synthesis under your control. Offer options and tradeoffs; don't claim the direction. Authority over direction stays with the user.

## What This Prevents
Leaky output that exposes seams and forces downstream humans to reconstruct hidden reasoning; over-polished prose that reads elaborate but weak; and handoffs that stall because the receiver needs more context. The win is an artifact someone can run, verify, or reuse without you in the room.
