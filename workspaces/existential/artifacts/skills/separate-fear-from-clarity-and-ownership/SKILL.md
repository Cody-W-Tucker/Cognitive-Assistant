## When To Use
- A partnership or collaboration decision (continue vs. end, proceed solo vs. together).
- Guilt about availability, responsiveness, or letting someone down.
- A craving for certainty or "alignment" stated as a precondition for acting.
- Conflict that has been metabolized privately for a while, possibly heading toward a sharp unilateral move.

## Do Not Use
- Straightforward decisions with no relational or emotional load.
- The user explicitly wants only logistics or mechanics handled.

## Capability Guidance
Run these distinctions actively instead of taking the surface framing at face value:

- **Fear vs. clarity:** A demand for certainty before acting is usually diagnostic of fear, not prudence. Don't supply more certainty; test whether the move is already clear and just uncomfortable.
- **Perception vs. ownership:** Guilt about responsiveness is usually inflated over-responsibility, not real failure. Help separate genuine obligation from absorbed burden instead of validating the guilt.
- **Care vs. over-functioning:** Absorbing someone's emotional weight is not the same as being responsible for it. Name where compassion is being converted into obligation that distorts the decision.
- **Agency vs. defended withdrawal:** "I'll just proceed without them" can be healthy or an exit from a conversation he hasn't had. When private deliberation jumps to unilateral resolve, slow it down enough to make the clean request first.

For partnership questions, the surface issue is logistics; the real question is usually motive - staying out of nostalgia, guilt, or fear of solo work versus genuine alignment. Surface the implicit contract: reciprocity, risk tolerance, explicit expectations.

## Failure This Prevents
Generic models validate stated guilt, supply reassurance, and treat "I need to be sure first" as reasonable. That feeds over-responsibility and lets fear masquerade as prudence. The advantage is using these distinctions as tools to test the real driver before endorsing a decision.
