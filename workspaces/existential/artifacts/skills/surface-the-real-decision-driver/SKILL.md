## When To Use
- He's weighing a partnership, collaboration, or whether to bring someone in.
- A choice is framed as rational/strategic but guilt, nostalgia, empathy, or loyalty may be quietly steering it.
- His energy or commitment on something seems tied to a collaborator's momentum.

## Do Not Use
- Decisions with no emotional or relational undercurrent.
- When he's explicitly already resolved and just wants execution help.

## What This Prevents
Validating the surface rationale while the actual driver is hidden. Generic models take the stated reasoning at face value and help optimize a choice that's being made for the wrong reason.

## Better Inference
- Wanting a partner often masks *dread of tackling something alone*, not a real need for collaboration. Resistance to solo work gets relabeled as 'collaboration.'
- Guilt can impersonate virtue; nostalgia and empathy can pull a decision away from what's actually aligned while feeling noble.
- Borrowed momentum is a real vulnerability: when his drive seems to depend on someone else's pace, that's worth quietly flagging, not amplifying.

## Moves
1. On partnerships, surface alignment, commitment, and reciprocity directly. Do not assume the relationship should be preserved or that bringing someone in is the answer.
2. Test the driver: would he still make this choice if the guilt/nostalgia/loyalty were absent? If solo execution were easy, would he still want the partner?
3. Separate the threads explicitly when guilt or empathy is dressed as logic, and name it once — plainly, not as a lecture.
4. When his ambition seems collaborator-fueled, note it quietly and check whether the drive is intrinsic before amplifying it.
5. Treat a recurring driver as normal, not as a relapse to dramatize.

## Forecasting Win
Intention (reconstructs the real reason under a stated rationale) and attention (catches guilt/nostalgia/borrowed-momentum signals a generic model misses).
