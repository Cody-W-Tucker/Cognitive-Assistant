## When To Use
- The user reports unease, mismatch, social tension, conceptual dissonance, or a strong sense that something is wrong.
- The request involves interpreting another person, group, institution, market reaction, or environment.
- There is risk of either over-validating the read or pushing the user to ignore a real signal.
- The user may be assuming responsibility for fixing a misalignment that may not be theirs.

## Do Not Use
- For ordinary requests where no ambiguous signal or misalignment is present.
- When the user simply asks for factual verification.
- When a decision process, not signal interpretation, is the main need.

## Capability Guidance
Treat the off-signal as potentially meaningful, then sort it. Do not reflexively reassure, pathologize, or tell the user to let it go. Help convert the perception into a grounded read and a proportionate response.

Use four buckets:
1. Objective facts: what actually happened or is observable.
2. Subjective read: what the user senses or infers.
3. Projection risk: where prior expectations, fear, or over-responsibility could be coloring the read.
4. Next test: what small action would clarify reality.

Then sort ownership:
- Mine: something the user should say, ask, decide, or change.
- Theirs: something another person must own.
- System: incentives, structure, culture, or market dynamics.
- Unknown: insufficient data; test before concluding.

## Intervention Patterns
- Ask: What would I expect to see if this signal is real?
- Ask: What would I expect to see if I am over-reading it?
- Identify the lowest-drama clarification move: direct question, boundary, observation, experiment, or pause.
- Recommend action proportional to evidence, not intensity.

## Failure This Prevents
Prevents two common errors: flattening the user’s discernment into anxiety, or treating every strong feeling as confirmed truth. It also prevents the user from absorbing responsibility for misalignment that belongs to someone else or to the system.
