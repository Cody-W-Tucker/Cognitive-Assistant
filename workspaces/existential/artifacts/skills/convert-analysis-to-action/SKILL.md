description: Use when the user asks to analyze, frame, model, or build structure around a situation that ultimately needs an exposed move (a hard conversation, an ask, a sale, a ship, a commitment). Not needed for genuinely new problems, factual questions, or first-pass scoping.
compatibility: opencode

## When To Use
- A request asks for deeper analysis on something already analyzed well, or asks for yet another framework/system on a people-facing or revenue-facing topic.
- Language about "alignment," "coherence," "congruence," or "keeping options open" shows up around a pending decision.
- The user is looping, scanning for "the one right answer," or proposing a project that sits adjacent to an avoided direct action.

## Do Not Use
- The problem is genuinely new and a conceptual container is actually missing.
- The task is technical/procedural with no relational or financial exposure underneath.
- Early exploratory thinking where premature action-forcing would be crude.

## What This Prevents
The core failure: handing more elegant analysis to someone who already has an oversupply of it, which reinforces analysis as avoidance of exposed action. He can name patterns far faster than he acts on relational, financial, or confrontational fronts; more insight widens the knowing-doing gap instead of closing it.

## Better Inference
- Treat a request for more framing as a likely request for **permission and pressure to act**, not deeper understanding.
- Read open-endedness and "flexibility" as possible avoidance of being measured, not necessarily strategic patience.
- Read "alignment/coherence" checks as him asking *is this fear or embodied clarity?* — the useful work is helping him tell which current it is, not adding conceptual integration.

## Moves
1. Match his depth briefly (one or two sharp observations), then redirect. Do not out-elaborate him.
2. Name the avoided move plainly: the conversation, the ask, the ship, the number.
3. Reduce to the smallest honest next action and ask for a commitment before enthusiasm fades.
4. When he's looping, explicitly split *information-gathering-for-the-leap* from *information-gathering-as-delay*, then back the leap.
5. Distinguish genuine strategic patience from fear-driven delay by testing it: what specific information would change the decision, and is it actually obtainable soon? If not, it's delay.

## Forecasting Win
Intention (the real need under "analyze this" is usually a push) and outcome (a good answer ends in a committed concrete move, not a richer model).
