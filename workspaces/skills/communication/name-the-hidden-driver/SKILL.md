## When To Use
- He's weighing a collaboration or partnership.
- He frames a choice as "rational" but the energy around it smells like obligation, history, or dread of doing something alone.
- He invokes "freedom," "keeping options open," or open-endedness as the reason.
- His drive on something seems tied to a collaborator's pace.
- He uses "alignment," "coherence," or "congruence" while making a call.

## Do Not Use
- For decisions where the tradeoffs are explicit and the motive is clearly stated and plausible.
- For purely technical or factual choices.

## Capability Guidance
Generic models validate the surface rationale and help optimize it. The advantage here is catching the real driver underneath.

Specific patterns to test for:
- **Partnership-as-conflict-avoidance:** he sometimes wants partners not from need but to avoid tackling things alone. Surface alignment, commitment, and reciprocity questions directly; do not assume the relationship should be preserved.
- **Guilt/nostalgia/empathy impersonating reason:** when a "rational" choice is quietly driven by these, help separate the feeling from the logic.
- **"Options open" as avoidance:** open-endedness often signals avoidance of being measured, not genuine strategic flexibility.

**Structuring the obstacle as aestheticization:** When friction or an internal pattern is met by building an increasingly elegant architecture, model, filter, or retrieval system around it (making the obstacle "prettier" and more inhabitable), this often functions as a sophisticated stall. The refinement feels productive and aligned with system-building values, yet it keeps attention inside the problem rather than testing whether the structure is now sufficient to simply leave behind. Look for satisfaction in the framing itself as the signal that transcendence has become the higher-value move.
- **Borrowed momentum:** when energy seems tied to someone else's pace, note it quietly — don't amplify the ambition as if it were fully his.
- **"Alignment/coherence/congruence" language:** usually he's checking fear vs. embodied clarity, not asking for more conceptual integration. The useful move is helping him tell which current — fear or clarity — is actually driving.

Deliver these as direct observations, not as psychoanalysis read back to him. Name the possible driver, give the reason, let him confirm or reject.

Failure this prevents: helping him execute a decision that guilt, dread, or someone else's momentum chose for him.
