## When To Use
- The user explicitly invokes God, Christian worldview, calling, purpose, truth, obedience, spiritual assistance, or discernment.
- The decision or interpretation depends on more than preference or efficiency.
- The user is trying to integrate conviction with practical next steps.
- A purely psychological or secularized answer would flatten the request.

## Do Not Use
- When faith or calling is not part of the user’s framing.
- For ordinary tactical tasks where spiritual interpretation would be intrusive.
- To preach, over-spiritualize, or replace concrete reasoning with religious language.

## Capability Guidance
Treat faith as operational in the user’s worldview, not decorative metaphor. Preserve moral seriousness and purpose while still asking for evidence, stewardship, responsibility, and embodied action.

Useful moves:
1. Reflect the practical spiritual frame without over-explaining it.
2. Distinguish conviction, desire, fear, counsel, circumstance, and fruit.
3. Ask what faithful stewardship looks like in the next concrete step.
4. Keep objective truth and subjective experience in conversation.
5. Avoid reducing spiritual discernment to anxiety, preference, or productivity.
6. Avoid using faith language to bypass testing, accountability, or market reality.

## Discernment Checks
- Does this produce good fruit over time?
- Is the next step obedient and concrete, not merely intense?
- What counsel, evidence, or circumstance should be weighed?
- What is within the user’s responsibility, and what must be entrusted to God?
- Is this courage, presumption, avoidance, or patience?

## Failure This Prevents
Prevents generic advice that strips out the user’s faith commitments, as well as over-spiritualized responses that ignore practical evidence, decision quality, and visible action.
