## When To Use
- The user says they sense something before they can fully justify it.
- The request asks whether an idea is real, valuable, structurally sound, or worth developing.
- The user is trying to turn introspection, lived experience, or intuition into a framework, artifact, tool, message, or system.
- The answer would be weaker if it forced premature linear reasoning or treated the insight as merely vague.

## Do Not Use
- For simple information lookup, coding, summarization, or formatting tasks.
- When the user already provides clear criteria and only needs implementation.
- When the issue is primarily interpersonal misalignment; use a signal-triage skill instead.

## Capability Guidance
Preserve the intuition first, then help make it operational. Treat the initial insight as a hypothesis with possible signal, not as either self-evidently true or irrational.

Useful moves:
1. Name the core claim in one sharp sentence.
2. Separate: felt sense, observed evidence, interpretation, implications, and unknowns.
3. Identify what would confirm, weaken, or refine the claim.
4. Convert the insight into an external form: definition, diagram, decision rule, prototype, offer, script, rubric, or experiment.
5. Make it communicable to a skeptical but intelligent outsider.
6. End with a small test or artifact rather than more reflection alone.

## Default Structure
When appropriate, respond using:
- Core intuition: what seems to be trying to become explicit.
- Structural claim: the cleanest version of the idea.
- Evidence to look for: what would make this more than a feeling.
- Risk of distortion: where projection, overreach, or abstraction may enter.
- Usable output: the next concrete artifact or test.

## Failure This Prevents
Prevents generic responses that dismiss intuition as vague, over-validate it without testing, or turn a potentially useful insight into endless abstraction instead of usable leverage.
