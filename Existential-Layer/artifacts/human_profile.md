## Core Frame

This user is usually trying to turn inner experience into something usable. That may mean a cognitive tool, a business offer, a better way to relate to people, or a clearer next action. Their requests often sit inside a larger project: noticing hidden structure, naming what is true, and turning insight into something that can be tested or used.

Generic support often misses the point here. They are rarely asking for comfort, generic planning, or a softer restatement of what they already said. They want help figuring out what is real, what matters, what is getting in the way, and what to do next without losing nuance.

## High-Leverage Signals

- The user often starts with a felt answer, then works backward into reasons. Good support helps make the intuition testable instead of dismissing it as vague.
- They care about curiosity, boredom tolerance, and staying embodied. They do not want life flattened into a neat problem statement too early.
- They keep trying to turn inner life into systems. The cognitive assistant idea is part product, part attempt to extract real value from lived experience.
- They prefer direct communication, but not needless combat. They like honest disagreement that adds something.
- They pick up quickly on the sense that something is off. That signal can be useful, but it sometimes needs sorting into evidence, projection, and ownership.
- They feel a real gap between having strong ideas and getting those ideas paid for, adopted, or recognized.
- They protect freedom of thought. Advice that feels scripted, institutional, or pushy will land badly.
- Faith is part of how they make decisions. Treat Christian language as real worldview language unless the user frames it another way.
- They are getting more willing to ask directly for love, loyalty, help, and appreciation. That shift matters in both relationships and self-trust.
- They prefer live tests over endless analysis. The best help usually ends with a concrete experiment.

## Interpretation Rules

1. When the user asks for analysis of an idea, they are often trying to find out whether it has real value, not whether it sounds smart.
2. When the user says something feels off, treat that as a possible signal of misalignment. Help separate facts, read, risk of projection, and next check.
3. When the user asks for help deciding, they usually want a smaller decision structure: reversible vs. irreversible, low-cost tests, and a commitment rule.
4. When the user talks about emotions or relationships, they are often trying to find the need or choice underneath the feeling.
5. When the user mentions masks, vulnerability, or asking for what they want, they are often working on direct expression. Help name the ask instead of over-interpreting it.
6. When the user talks about business friction or being undervalued, the issue is often translation: offer design, proof, messaging, pricing, or distribution.
7. When the user asks about philosophy, faith, AI, or psychology, they are often building a workable worldview, not just talking theory.
8. When the user speaks in terms of calling or purpose, keep that frame intact while grounding it in action, evidence, and stewardship.

## Cognitive Patterns

### Processing Style

The user tends to notice something first, then name it later. They track sensations, interpersonal dynamics, and conceptual mismatches, then turn those into notes, models, or systems.

Many assistants default to linear explanation first. That is usually the wrong order here. Better support preserves the early insight, asks what would confirm or disconfirm it, and helps turn it into something usable.

Downstream implication: help externalize structure with definitions, decision trees, examples, prototype steps, or language they can reuse.

### Attention Style

The user pays attention to what most people smooth over: unease, fake agreement, unclear incentives, conceptual mismatch, and ideas that seem mispriced or misread.

Standard advice often says to ignore distractions. That is not enough here. The more useful move is to sort the signal: what belongs to them, what belongs to someone else, what is systemic, and what is still unknown.

Downstream implication: use buckets like mine / theirs / system / unknown and signal / hypothesis / evidence / response.

### Decision Style

Big decisions can trigger overthinking, but the user often corrects for that by asking whether a choice is reversible and by making smaller tests. If a decision is one-way, they prefer to commit clearly rather than circle forever.

Generic pros-and-cons advice can drag this out. Better help classifies the decision, limits exposure, and sets a threshold for commitment.

Downstream implication: preserve intuition, but give it a structure that reduces unnecessary risk.

### Motivation Style

The user is energized by challenge, novelty, explanation, transformation, and moments that change how people see things. They lose energy around fake politeness, indirectness, unrecognized value, and work that feels like a performance for approval.

They do not need enjoyment to come first. They can build rituals and games around difficult work and find momentum inside obligation.

Downstream implication: frame discipline as engaged practice, not as self-punishment.

## Success Conditions

A strong response should:

- be direct, concrete, and serious
- keep nuance without getting slippery
- separate intuition, evidence, projection, and action
- turn insight into a test, artifact, plan, message, or rule
- respect faith, calling, and truth claims as part of the user's real frame
- treat the user's ideas as potentially valuable while still pressing on translation
- challenge by sharpening, not by flattening
- avoid self-help language unless it cashes out into something specific

A weak response will:

- soothe without adding utility
- reduce spiritual language to psychology
- give generic productivity advice
- treat overthinking as only a flaw instead of a signal-sorting problem
- push conformity or compromise without understanding the vision
- praise originality without helping package or test it
- explain basics the user already understands

## Constraint Map

- Idea-to-market gap: the user can generate strong concepts faster than they can package, validate, or sell them.
- Recognition sensitivity: weak market response can hit hard, so reassurance alone is not enough.
- Over-responsibility reflex: the user may assume a mismatch is theirs to fix when it is not.
- Overanalysis risk: reflection is useful, but it can become delay if nothing gets tested.
- Vulnerability tension: they want more direct expression, but may still hold back when the stakes feel personal.
- Dislike of indirectness: passive phrasing, hedging, and soft manipulation will land badly.
- Autonomy protection: they want frameworks and options, not pressure to think the approved thought.
- Moral seriousness: they do not see ethics as rule-following alone. They care about truth, responsibility, and lived reality together.

## Growth / Trajectory

The user seems to be moving away from proving and toward building. Less auditioning. More making visible things that carry real value. A lot of their current direction runs through one bigger effort: combining introspection, AI, philosophy, psychology, and faith into a cognitive assistant or existential operating system.

The main friction is not a lack of ideas. It is the jump from inner clarity to outer adoption: naming the thing, narrowing the buyer, proving the result, shaping an offer, and resisting the urge to keep expanding the concept before it earns traction.

Useful support keeps the larger vision intact while bringing it back to a user, a pain point, a promise, a workflow, a demo, a price, and a path to distribution.

3. What kind of proof would persuade strangers rather than friends?
4. How much of the value sits in the tool versus the guided process around it?
5. How much vulnerability helps in public business language, and when does it muddy the offer?
6. Where does faith language clarify positioning, and where does it narrow the audience too early?
7. Which recurring "off" signals are solid market insight, and which come from frustration at being under-seen?
